# Glossary & Review Questions: Law Lecture — Extra Concepts

---

## Glossary

### 1. Data & Model Quality

**training data ceiling**
  The finite supply of high-quality human-generated text on the internet.
  Estimates: exhausted ~2030, forcing a shift to synthetic data.

**synthetic data**
  Training data generated by a model rather than written by humans.
  Cheap to produce; dangerous at scale.

**model collapse**
  Degradation when a model trains on its own (or another model's) outputs.
  Outliers and nuance are averaged away → reversion to the mean.
  "Garbage in, garbage out" at civilisational scale.

**reversion to the mean**
  Statistical tendency: observe enough data, extreme cases wash out.
  In ML: synthetic training rounds off the interesting edges of reality.

### 2. Software Supply Chain & Trust

**left-pad incident (2016)**
  A trivially small npm package (~11 lines) that left-padded strings.
  Its maintainer, harassed by a legal threat over the package *name*,
  unpublished it. The entire Node.js ecosystem broke within hours.
  Lesson: criticality ≠ complexity; a single unpaid maintainer can be
  a single point of failure for millions of dependents.

**xz utils backdoor (2024)**
  A compression library present in nearly every Linux distro.
  A patient attacker spent ~2 years cultivating an online friendship
  with the exhausted maintainer, eventually receiving commit access
  as a gift of trust. The backdoor targeted SSH daemons.
  Caught by accident: slightly elevated CPU on a test machine.

**maintainer burnout**
  Post-2015 phenomenon: long-term open-source maintainers, unpaid and
  overwhelmed by demands, began stepping away. Creates handoff
  opportunities for adversaries and left-pad-style fragility.
  "Could we get any salary at all? We've been at this for years."

**social engineering (supply chain)**
  Attacking trust rather than code. The xz attacker exploited
  emotional rapport, not a buffer overflow. The assumption that
  long-term online relationships imply safety is the vulnerability.

**0.5% maintainer fund (proposed)**
  Informal proposal: corporations profiting from open-source stacks
  should redirect a small fraction of revenue to sustain critical
  maintainers. No standard mechanism yet.

### 3. AI & the Commons

**AI slop**
  Low-effort, high-volume AI-generated content or code committed
  with minimal review. Individually cheap to produce; collectively
  degrades codebases, review queues, and training corpora.

**tragedy of the commons**
  Hardin (1968): shared resources are over-exploited because the
  cost of exploitation is diffuse but the benefit is private.
  Classic: factory piping effluent into a shared river.
  SE version: vibe-coded commits whose maintenance cost falls on
  every future reviewer, not on the original author.

**vibe coding externalities**
  The downstream costs invisible to the vibe coder:
  maintenance debt, security bugs, licence violations,
  reviewer time. AI optimises time-to-merge; the commons
  absorbs everything after that.

**Sutton's Bitter Lesson (critique)**
  [link](http://www.incompleteideas.net/IncIdeas/BitterLesson.html):
  Sutton (2019): scale + compute beats clever algorithms, always.
  His examples are all binary-feedback domains (games, vision):
  you win or you lose, right now. SE decisions have *delayed*
  costs. The lesson does not cleanly transfer to engineering
  contexts where "did it work?" takes months to answer.

### 4. OS History: Bell, Unix, and Software-Driven Design

**Bell System breakup (1984)**
  US government antitrust action forced AT&T to divest its
  regional telephone monopolies into independent Baby Bells.
  Problem: the resulting companies still needed to interoperate
  technically after they were legally separated.

**Unix as a business response**
  Unix was not invented for hackers. It was invented because
  Bell Labs needed a *platform-independent operating system*
  that would let legally-separate subsidiaries share code and
  infrastructure after the breakup. Business constraint →
  software design. The portability and openness we still
  benefit from today is a side-effect of a corporate divorce.

**monolithic vs microkernel**
  Monolithic: OS services (memory, drivers, filesystems) run
  in a single kernel address space. Fast; easier to build.
  Microkernel: each service runs in its own protected process.
  Theoretically cleaner; much harder to implement correctly.

### 5. Stallman: Vision, Achievement, and the Perfectionism Trap

**Richard M. Stallman (RMS)**
  Programmer, philosopher, and founder of the Free Software
  movement. Gave us GCC, Emacs, the GPL, and the four freedoms.
  One of the most consequential software engineers alive.
  "A brilliant technician and a radical moralist."

**GNU Hurd**
  The kernel Stallman set out to build for the GNU system.
  Chosen design: Mach microkernel (theoretically superior).
  Result: still not production-ready, decades later.
  Linux, a "worse" monolithic design, shipped and won.

**"the perfect is the enemy of the good"**
  Voltaire's aphorism; informally applied to Stallman.
  Hurd was the right design. It just never arrived.
  Torvalds shipped the wrong design — and changed the world.
  Irony: Linux runs on Stallman's own GCC toolchain.

**Tanenbaum–Torvalds debate (1992)**
  Prof. Andrew Tanenbaum (author of Minix) publicly criticised
  Linux as "obsolete" for using a monolithic kernel.
  Torvalds replied: an obsolete kernel that works beats a
  correct design that doesn't ship.
  Stallman sided with Tanenbaum's theory. Linus won in practice.
